Terminating Employment Agreements

Generally, one has to look at both the employment agreement and the Employment Act when terminating an employment agreement.

Various factors determine how an employment agreement can be terminated, such as the terms of the employment agreement and whether the Employment Act (“EA”) applies to the said employee.

In most instances, an employment agreement is terminated under one of the following:

  1. Termination with notice;
  2. Termination without notice (by payment in lieu of notice);
  3. Termination without notice (for cause);
  4. Termination due to redundancy, retrenchment or reorganisation; and
  5. Termination by expiry.

(1) Termination with notice

If the employment agreement specifies a notice period, notice will have to be given according to the mechanism set out in the contract. Generally, either the employer or the employee may terminate the employment agreement by giving notice to the other.

If the EA applies, then:

  • the notice must be in writing;
  • the period of the notice must include the day on which notice is given;
  • payment of all outstanding salary and other sums due to the employee must be made on the dismissal date (or, if this is not possible, within 3 days thereafter, not being a rest day or public holiday or other holiday);
  • the notice period must be the same for both the employer and employee; and

  • the notice period cannot be less than the following:

    Length of Employment Minimum Notice Period
    Less than 26 weeks 1 day
    26 weeks to less than 2 years 1 week
    2 Years to less than 5 years 2 weeks
    5 years and above 4 weeks

During the notice period, some employers may also require that the employee go on garden leave. Garden leave is usually required only for employees in sensitive positions. During the garden leave period, the employee is typically not allowed to go into work and may be prohibited from making contact with any of the employer's customers or suppliers.

(2) Termination without notice (by payment in lieu of notice)

Either the employer or the employee may terminate an employment agreement without waiting for the expiry of the notice period by paying to the other party salary in lieu of notice.

The amount to pay is calculated as follows:

  • the sum equal to the amount of salary at the gross rate of pay which would have accrued to the employee during the notice period; or
  • in the case of a monthly-rated employee, where the notice period is less than 1 month, the amount payable for any 1 day will be the gross rate of pay for 1 day’s work.

Payment in lieu of notice can be given even if notice has already been served.

(3) Termination without notice (for cause)

Either the employee or the employer may terminate an employment agreement without notice in the event of any wilful breach by the other party of a condition in the employment agreement.

This is sometimes called termination ‘for cause’.

What constitutes ‘for cause’ is usually set out in the employment agreement, which typically include things such as the employee:

  • engaging in any serious misconduct, dishonesty, bribery or illegal gratuities;
  • being convicted of a criminal offence (other than road traffic offences);
  • becoming a bankrupt;
  • becoming of unsound mind; or
  • not being able to legally work in Singapore (for example, withdrawal of employment pass).

An employer is deemed to have broken the employment agreement if he/she fails to pay salary in accordance with the EA.

An employee is deemed to have broken the employment agreement if he/she is absent from work for more than 2 days continuously without prior leave from the employer and he/she:

  • has no reasonable excuse for the absence; or
  • does not inform nor attempt to inform the employer of the excuse for the absence.

(4) Termination due to redundancy, retrenchment or reorganisation

Under the EA, there are no specific requirements for termination due to redundancy, retrenchment or reorganisation. Generally, an employer can terminate an employment agreement by either:

  • giving notice to the employee; or
  • giving payment in lieu of notice.

Singapore's courts have stated that although the EA states that employees in continuous service for less than 2 years are not entitled to any retrenchment benefits, this does not mean that employees in continuous service of more than 2 years are automatically entitled to retrenchment benefits.

Several other issues and documents need to be looked at to determine if retrenchment benefits must be given to particular employees, such as:

  • the terms of the employment agreement;
  • the terms of the collective agreement (if any) for trade union members;
  • the employee handbook or manual; and
  • any prevailing policy of the employer on retrenchment benefits.

Even if retrenchment benefits is found to not be required to be given, employers may still choose to make ex-gratia payments to retrenched employees.

In carrying out a retrenchment exercise, Singapore's Ministry of Manpower (“MOM”) encourages employers to:

  • inform MOM before carrying out any retrenchment exercise; and
  • inform affected employees of impending retrenchment before giving the formal notice of retrenchment.

(5) Termination by expiry

Employment agreements may be for a fixed-term, and include an end date for the employment. Upon the end date, the employment agreement will automatically terminate.
