Appointing New Directors

For Singapore companies, the procedure for appointing new directors is generally set out in the company's constitution.

In Singapore companies, directors are the people responsible for the day-to-day management of the company. There are also certain duties imposed on directors, and failure to comply with such duties may open them up to certain liabilities.

Subject to certain prohibitions, Singapore private companies are generally allowed to appoint new directors at any time.

Pre-Appointment Considerations

Before appointing any new directors, it is important that:

  • the company is not restricted from appointing the new directors;
  • the new directors qualify to act as directors; and
  • the new directors consent to act as directors of the company.

Company Restrictions on Appointing New Directors

Certain restrictions may be imposed on the company preventing the new directors from being appointed, such as:

  • the existence of a prescribed maximum number of directors in the company;
  • any professional qualification which all (or a proportion) of the directors must hold (for example, in engineering companies or law corporations);
  • any requirement that directors must hold certain number of shares in the company (the ‘shareholding qualification’); and
  • any requirement that all (or a proportion) of the directors must be appointed by a certain person.

The following documents and information are usually examined to determine if any such restrictions exist:

  • the company’s constitution;
  • the company’s minute books;
  • shareholders agreement (if any);
  • nature of the company’s business; and
  • any relevant legislation governing companies in such businesses.

New Directors’ Qualification to Act as Directors

Directors must fulfil all of the following criteria to be able to act as a director in any Singapore company:

  • be at least eighteen (18) years of age;
  • have full mental capacity;
  • not be an undischarged bankrupt (anywhere in the world);
  • not have any disqualification order made by the High Court of Singapore against the new director currently in force;
  • not be disqualified under Singapore’s Limited Liability Partnerships Act from being a manager of a limited liability partnership; and
  • in the past 5 years:
    • not been convicted of any offence involving fraud or dishonesty punishable with imprisonment for three (3) months or more;
    • not been convicted of three (3) or more offences under Singapore’s Companies Act relating to the requirements of giving notice of any matter or filing any returns, accounts or other documents with Singapore’s Registrar of Companies; and
    • not had three (3) or more orders of the High Court of Singapore made against the new director in relation to the aforesaid requirements.

All new directors are required to sign Form 45, which incorporates a statement stating all of the above.

New Directors’ Consent to Act as Directors

Before appointing any new directors, each of the new directors must have consented to act as a director of the company.

This consent can be evidenced by each of the new directors signing Form 45, which also incorporates a declaration of consent.

Procedure for Appointing New Directors

There is no prescribed manner under Singapore’s Companies Act for the appointment of new directors in private companies. The procedure for the appointment of directors is usually found in the company’s constitution.

Typically, the appointment of new directors is either:

  • by the existing directors; or
  • by the shareholders.

Effective Date of Appointment

The effective date of the new director’s appointment will be specified in the resolution, typically either from the date of the resolution or the date of the consent to act (whichever is later).

Post-Appointment Matters

Upon appointment of any new directors, the company must lodge with ACRA such appointment within 1 month of the date of appointment.

This is a requirement under section 173(6) of Singapore’s Companies Act, and failure to lodge with ACRA within the required timeframe will cause the company and every officer of the company who is in default to be liable to a fine of up to SGD5,000 and also to a default penalty.
