What is a Company Charge?

A company charge is a type of security over assets given by a company to a lender.

When a company borrows money, the lender (for example, the bank) will typically require that security be given for the loan.

One common form of security lenders require is a charge over the company’s assets and/or sale proceeds.

What is the effect of a charge?

For the chargee

The chargee is the beneficiary of the charge, and is typically the lender (for example, banks) or creditor of the company.

Having a charge over the company’s assets allows the chargee to have priority on such assets ahead of the ordinary creditors of the company should the company default on its debts.

For the chargor

The chargor is the company granting the charge.

A charge is a non-possessory security, meaning that the company can remain in possession of the assets over which there is a charge.

A charge also does not transfer ownership. However, a charge will act as an encumbrance on the charged assets.

Once a charge is registered with ACRA, it will show up on all public searches performed on the company.

What types of charges are there?

There are two main types of charges:

  • fixed charge; and
  • floating charge.

Fixed charge

A fixed charge is a charge over specific assets (for example, specific pieces of land or vehicles).

Typically if there is a fixed charge over an asset, the company cannot deal with or dispose of the asset unless the chargee either (i) gives their consent; or (ii) lifts the charge.

A chargee typically will not lift the charge unless the debt in respect of the charge is satisfied.

Floating charge

A floating charge is a charge over a class of assets (as opposed to a specific asset). Examples of such classes of assets include all the inventory of the company, or all the vehicles owned by the company.

The benefit of a floating charge is that the company remains free to deal with or dispose the class of assets, until the floating charge crystalises. For example, the company is still free to acquire and dispose of its inventory up until crystalisation.

Crystalisation of a floating charge typically occurs on certain events of default happening. Such events of default are contained within the charge document.

A floating charge becomes a fixed charge upon crystalisation.

When must a charge be registered with ACRA?

Under section 131 of Singapore’s Companies Act, a charge must be registered within 30 days of its creation by filing with ACRA:

  • a statement containing the particulars of the charge; and
  • the instrument creating the charge.

If a charge is not registered within 30 days of its creation, an application to the Singapore courts must be made to extend the time for registration.

What kind of charges must be registered with ACRA?

Under section 131(3) of the Companies Act, the charge being registered must be one of the following:

  • a charge to secure any issue of debentures;
  • a charge on uncalled share capital of a company;
  • a charge on shares of a subsidiary of a company which are owned by the company;
  • a charge or an assignment created or evidenced by an instrument which if executed by an individual would require registration as a bill of sale;
  • a charge on land wherever situate or any interest therein;
  • a charge on book debts of the company;
  • a floating charge on the undertaking or property of a company;
  • a charge on calls made but not paid;
  • a charge on ship or aircraft or any share in a ship or aircraft; and
  • a charge on goodwill, on a patent or licence under a patent, on a trade mark, or on a copyright or a licence under a copyright.

Who can register a charge with ACRA?

If a charge is required to be registered under section 131 of the Companies Act, either of the following party may register such related charge documents and particulars with ACRA:

  • the company concerned; or
  • any person interested in the documents.

However, it is usually the company that registers the charge as it is the company (and every of its officer who is in default) that will be liable in the event of default in compliance with section 131 of the Companies Act.

What happens if a charge is not registered with ACRA?

If a charge is not registered with ACRA in accordance with section 131 of the Companies Act:

  • the charge shall be void against the liquidator and any creditor of the company, provided that where a charge becomes so void, the money secured thereby shall immediately become payable; and
  • the company and every officer of the company who is in default is guilty of an offence and will be liable on conviction to a fine not exceeding SGD1,000 and also to a default penalty.

When must a satisfaction of a charge be registered with ACRA?

When a charge is satisfied, the chargee (that is, the lender) must evidence the satisfaction by endorsing a statement of the payment, satisfaction, release or ceasing of:

  • the debt for which the charge was given has been paid or satisfied in whole or in part; or
  • the property or undertaking charged or any part thereof has been released from the charge or has ceased to form part of the company’s property or undertaking of the company concerned.

With this statement, the company can then proceed to file the satisfaction with ACRA and remove the charge from the company’s register of charges.

The Companies Act is silent on the timeframe for lodging a charge that is satisfied with ACRA. However, as a matter of good practice, companies should lodge a satisfaction of charge with ACRA as soon as it is satisfied, so that the encumbrance over the company’s assets is lifted.
