Incorporating a Private Limited Company in Singapore

Incorporating a private limited company in Singapore is relatively straightforward and can generally be incorporated within the day (provided that the necessary information and documents are in order).

To incorporate a private limited company in Singapore, the following information and documents are generally required:

  1. Proposed Company Name;
  2. Business Activity;
  3. Initial Paid-up Capital;
  4. First Shareholders;
  5. First Directors;
  6. Registered Office Address;
  7. Company Secretary; and
  8. Company Constitution.

(1) Proposed Company Name

Before incorporating a Singapore company, the proposed company name must be approved by and reserved with the Accounting and Corporate Regulatory Authority of Singapore (“ACRA”).

A name reservation with ACRA can be filed online and will generally be approved within two (2) days (sometimes within hours of submission), but may take up to two (2) months if further approvals are required by ACRA or other Governmental authorities.

Once the proposed company name is approved, it will be reserved for an initial period of two (2) months. This is extendable for one (1) more month. During this period whereby the proposed company name is reserved, the company may be incorporated.

Read more: Guidelines for Proposed Company Names in Singapore

(2) Business Activity

Singapore companies must register their business activity with ACRA.

A company must have a minimum of one (1) business activity and a maximum of two (2) business activities. The business activity must be one found in the Singapore Standard Industrial Classification (“SSIC”).

(3) Initial Paid-up Capital

Paid-up capital is the amount of money that the company receives from its shareholders as a result of issuing new shares.

The minimum paid-up capital for a Singapore company is SGD1.00 and the minimum number of issued shares is one (1).

(4) First Shareholders

Shareholders are usually seen as the owners of the company and may have certain rights under the Companies Act and general law, such as the right to attend and vote at general meetings.

However, shareholders generally cannot interfere with the management of the company as the power to manage the company is usually vested with the board of directors.

A Singapore company must have at least one (1) shareholder and may generally be 100%-owned by foreign natural or legal persons (for example, corporate entities).

If the shareholder is a natural person, he/she must be at least eighteen (18) years old.

(5) First Directors

The managing of a company’s business is in the board of directors’ hands (and not the shareholders).

Usually, the board of directors are collectively given the power to exercise all powers of the company other than those prohibited by the Companies Act and the company’s Constitution. Directors are bound by certain statutory and common law duties to the company.

A Singapore company must have at least one (1) director, at least one (1) of which must be ordinarily resident in Singapore (that is, either a Singapore citizen, Singapore permanent resident or Singapore employment pass holder). The rest of the directors may be foreigners.

Each of the directors must also be:

  • natural persons (that is, human beings);
  • at least eighteen (18) years old;
  • not a bankrupt; and
  • not disqualified from acting as a director.

(6) Registered Office Address

All Singapore companies must from the date of its incorporation:

  • have a registered office within Singapore to which all communications and notices may be addressed; and
  • which must be open and accessible to the public for not less than three (3) hours during ordinary business hours on each business day.

(7) Company Secretary

A company secretary ensures that a company, its officers and its shareholders follow due process and comply with applicable law, rules and regulations.

Every Singapore company must have a company secretary, and this position cannot be vacant for more than six (6) months at any one time.

The company secretary must have his/her principal or only place of residence in Singapore and must be present at the registered office of the company by himself/herself or his/her agent or clerk on the days and at the hours during which the registered office is to be accessible to the public.

However, if the company is private, the company secretary, his/her agent or clerk need not be physically present at the registered office during the times specified so long as the company secretary, his/her agent or clerk is readily contactable by a person at the registered office by telephone or other means of instantaneous communication during those times.

(8) Company Constitution

The Constitution of a company is an important document which sets out the rights, powers, duties and obligations of and between the company, its directors and its shareholders. Every Singapore company must have a Constitution.

The Constitution may either be:

  • in the form of the Model Constitution provided under the Companies Act (that is, the default Constitution) in force at the time of adoption;
  • in the form of the Model Constitution provided under the Companies Act (that is, the default Constitution) in force from time to time; or
  • customised.

The Constitution may be amended after incorporation, and must be filed with ACRA. The Constitution is a public document available for public inspection.
