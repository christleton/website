Types of Business Entities in Singapore

The type of business entity you choose will affect your personal liability, tax liability, reporting requirements (paperwork), financing ability and other matters.

Generally speaking, there are three (3) main types of business entities most commonly used to conduct business in Singapore:

  1. Sole Proprietorship;
  2. Partnership; and
  3. Private Limited Company.

(1) Sole Proprietorship

A sole proprietorship is typically used for low risk small businesses operated by a single person.

Advantages of a Sole Proprietorship

The main advantages of a sole proprietorship are:

  • relatively simpler process of setting up;
  • lower compliance requirements (less paperwork); and
  • possibly a lower tax rate as it is taxed at the owner’s personal tax rate, but not that much lower.

Disadvantages of a Sole Proprietorship

The main disadvantages of a sole proprietorship are:

  • unlimited personal liability (a huge disadvantage);
  • impossible to raise funds by equity;
  • possible difficulties in raising funds by debt; and
  • complications in the event of a merger or acquisition (that is, a sale of the business).

Having unlimited personal liability means that if someone sues the sole proprietorship and wins, the owner has to pay for everything. This puts sole proprietors at the risk of personal bankruptcy if something goes wrong. In a limited company, the shareholders’ maximum losses are the money they initially put into the company (that is, the paid-up capital).

If someone wants to buy out the sole proprietorship, the merger and acquisition transaction is likely to be more complicated than if the sole proprietorship was a company, as each individual asset and contract that is being acquired has to be individually identified and included in the asset sale agreement. The contracts being acquired will also have to be assigned or novated to the purchaser.

Structure of a Sole Proprietorship

Legal Personality Sole proprietorships have no separate legal personality.
Perpetuity Exists only for the lifetime of the owner.
Ownership Can only be owned by one (1) individual person or company.
Equity Fund Raising Equity fund raising in a sole proprietorship is impossible, as unlike a company, there is no concept of ‘shares’ and only one person can own a sole proprietorship (the ‘sole’ in sole proprietorship).
Debt Fund Raising Raising funds by borrowing money in a sole proprietorship is equivalent to personally borrowing money, and the difficulty in raising funds by debt is dependant on the owner’s ability to borrow money.
Management Owner has absolute say in the running of the business.
Asset Ownership Assets are owned in the owner’s personal name.
Closing Down Relatively straightforward.

Ongoing Compliance for a Sole Proprietorship

Accounts Accounts need not be lodged with ACRA.
Audit Sole proprietorships need not have their accounts audited.
Annual Return Sole proprietorships need not file annual returns with ACRA.
Authority to Act No requirement to pass resolutions authorising transactions before they are entered into.

Liability Considerations for a Sole Proprietorship

Personal The main downside of a sole proprietorship is that there is unlimited personal liability.
Taxation Profits made by a sole proprietorship are taxed at the owner’s personal tax rate.
Tax Exemptions Any tax relief will be the owner’s personal tax relief.

(2) Partnership

A partnership is an expanded form of a sole proprietorship, allowing more than one person to organise together to conduct business.

The parties forming a partnership must enter into a Partnership Agreement, which will govern their relationship and serves as the ‘constitution’ of the partnership.

There are 3 main types of partnerships:

  1. General Partnership;
  2. Limited Partnership (“LP”); and
  3. Limited Liability Partnerships (“LLP”).

(3) Private Limited Company

A private limited company is the most common form of vehicle used for conducting business in Singapore.

Governed by Singapore's Companies Act, private limited companies typically have the words ‘Pte. Ltd.’ (the abbreviated form of ‘Private Limited’) or ‘Ltd.’ (the abbreviated form of ‘Limited’) at the end of their names.

The two (2) types of private limited companies available are:

  • Private Company Limited by Shares (most common); and
  • Private Company Limited by Guarantee (very uncommon).

A ‘Private Company Limited by Shares’ is the most common form of company, as the shareholders cannot be asked to pay more than the amount unpaid (if any) on their shares when the company is wound up. If the shares have all been fully paid-up, then the shareholders cannot be asked for any further contribution and their liability is limited at the amount they have paid-up for the shares. The maximum the shareholders can lose are their investment in the company. The company cannot go after the shareholders' personal asset to satisfy corporate debts or liabilities.

A ‘Private Company Limited by Guarantee’ is very uncommon and are typically not business trading companies, but companies generally engaged in charitable, scientific, religious or artistic activities (for example, the Singapore Zoo). The shareholders in a private company limited by guarantee cannot be asked to pay more than the amount they guarantee, which is stated in the private company limited by guarantee's constitution.

We will only be discussing the private company limited by shares in this article, which will be referred to as the “Private Limited Company”.

Advantages of a Private Limited Company

The main advantages of a Private Limited Company are:

  • separate legal personality distinct from its shareholders and directors;
  • owners not personally liable for any debts or losses of the company;
  • possible to raise funds via equity;
  • relative ease of raising funds via debt;
  • ownership can be easily transferred;
  • new owners can be easily added; and
  • company can survive the lifetime of its owners, in perpetuity.

Disadvantages of a Private Limited Company

The main disadvantages of a Private Limited Company are:

  • more costly to set up and maintain;
  • more stringent ongoing compliance requirements;
  • must appoint a company secretary; and
  • must appoint an auditor (unless exempted).

Exempt Private Company

A private company may be exempt or non-exempt.

The benefits of an exempt private company are:

  • no need to file with ACRA a balance sheet and profit and loss account with its annual return (but they still have to be prepared); and
  • ability to make loans to its directors and to companies in which its directors are interested.

The requirements to qualify as an exempt private company are:

  • does not have any corporate shareholder (owned only by natural persons); and
  • has less than 20 shareholders.

Structure of a Private Limited Company

Legal Personality Legal personality separate from its owners.
Perpetuity Can exist in perpetuity (forever).
Ownership Minimum of one (1) shareholder and maximum of fifty (50) shareholders.
  100% foreign ownership generally allowed.
  Shareholders can be natural or legal persons (human beings or corporations).
  If natural person, must be at least 18 years old.
Equity Fund Raising Possible to raise funds by issuing new shares.
  Ease of adding investors as new shareholders.
Debt Fund Raising Possible to raise funds by borrowing in the company's name.
Management Management control is separate from ownership.
  Management decisions made by the board of directors.
  Minimum of one (1) locally resident director.
Asset Ownership Can own assets in the company's own name.
Closing Down More complicated process of closing down.

Ongoing Compliance for a Private Limited Company

Secretary Must appoint a company secretary (see Company Secretary).
Accounts Must prepare accounts.
  If exempt private company, need not file accounts with ACRA (but must still prepare them).
Audit Unless exempted, the company must be audited.
Annual Return Must file annual return with ACRA.
Authority to Act Must pass resolutions to authorise certain acts, such as entering into certain contracts or opening a bank account.

Liability Considerations for a Private Limited Company

Personal Assuming no fraud or other serious misdeeds, generally the maximum that a shareholder can lose is the amount of investment put into the company.
  Assuming no fraud or other serious misdeeds, the company cannot go after the shareholders' personal assets to satisfy corporate debts or liabilities.
Taxation Profits of the company are taxed at the corporate rate.
Tax Exemptions Tax relief that are given to companies apply.
